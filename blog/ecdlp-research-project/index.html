Understanding ECDLP: A Research Project on the Hardness of Elliptic Curve Cryptography

February 05, 2026 ecdlp, secp256k1, cryptography, research

Introduction

Elliptic curves are one of the pillars of modern cryptography. The security of many systems—including Bitcoin—rests on the difficulty of the so-called Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP).

Formally, given a base point \(G\) on an elliptic curve and a public point \[Q = [d]G\] for some unknown integer \(d\), the task is to recover the value of \(d\). For sufficiently large parameters, this problem is considered computationally intractable: no known classical (non-quantum) algorithm can solve ECDLP on a general curve in time polynomial in \(\log n\), where \(n\) is the group order.

The best known generic approach, Pollard’s rho algorithm, has time complexity on the order of \(O(\sqrt{n})\). For the 256-bit curve secp256k1, this means roughly \(2^{128} \approx 3.4 \times 10^{38}\) group operations—completely beyond the capabilities of current hardware.

In other words, breaking a 256-bit key through brute force or generic algorithms is practically impossible. Any potential attack must exploit some special structure of the curve or additional information.

Project Goals

Our project does not aim to practically break the cryptography of Bitcoin or other systems. Instead, we seek a deeper mathematical understanding of ECDLP—we want to explore why this problem is so difficult and what structures, algorithms, or models it conceals.

The main goal is to extend theoretical knowledge of ECDLP over elliptic curves, with special attention to secp256k1. We are not looking for practical vulnerabilities for immediate attacks on real cryptosystems; rather, we are interested in the mathematical essence of the problem.

We examine a wide range of aspects of elliptic curves and discrete logarithms:

  • Classical curve models: Weierstrass, Edwards, Montgomery, and others

  • Isogenies: mappings between curves and their computational implications

  • Arithmetic methods: efficient algorithms for point operations

  • Automorphisms: internal symmetries and their exploitation

  • Special structures: curves with \(j\)-invariant 0, complex multiplication (CM)

The goal is to describe and unify knowledge about all known—and lesser-known—layers of ECDLP: how different models and coordinate systems affect computational complexity, how curves can be transformed via isogenies, what endomorphisms exist and how they can speed up computations, and so on.

For example, the secp256k1 curve, thanks to complex multiplication (the so-called Gallant–Lambert–Vanstone endomorphism), allows scalar multiplication implementations to be accelerated by approximately 30%. However, an attacker can exploit the same advantage, so this special structure does not compromise security by itself. This improves constant factors but does not change the asymptotic hardness of ECDLP. (The SafeCurves project notes it as a potential risk for a possible future breakthrough, but no attack exploiting this additional structure is currently known.)

Our project systematically examines all such possibilities and limits of ECDLP on elliptic curves—the aim is to leave nothing out and get to the core of the problem.

Research Values

We emphasize formal correctness and proofs. Every claim is supported by rigorous mathematical argumentation—instead of speculation, we provide proofs or clear justification.

At the same time, we conduct reproducible experiments: we develop and publish algorithm prototypes, measure their performance on real data, and make results available to the community.

Negative results are just as important as positive ones—systematic refutation of dead ends is part of the project. If some attack direction or optimization does not lead to the goal, we thoroughly document why it failed so others can learn from it.

All outputs will be openly published for the community—in the form of research reports, blog articles, and freely available code in repositories. This open-source and open-science approach is meant to support the credibility of results and enable others to build upon them.

Where We Are Heading

In the longer term, we ask the question: what constitutes the “fundamental barrier” of ECDLP?

Why, after decades of research, do we not know a classical algorithm significantly faster than \(\sqrt{n}\)? Is there some deeper geometric or algebraic reason why ECDLP is so resistant to speedup?

In our project, we try to map the “space of all possible speedups”—that is, all known approaches to potentially facilitate solving the discrete logarithm. This includes:

  • Traditional methods: Pohlig–Hellman decomposition for groups with easily factorable order, index calculus in various variants, exploitation of small embedding degree via MOV attack, etc.

  • New and less explored ideas: use of summation polynomials, search for special curve parameterizations, machine learning over point structures, etc.

We are interested in whether there is a chance for a sub-\(\sqrt{n}\) breakthrough—an algorithm that would solve ECDLP faster than \(O(\sqrt{n})\)—and, if not, why such a breakthrough apparently does not exist.

We will investigate whether geometric transformations (isogenies, special projections) or algebraic properties (complex multiplication, special points with low order, higher automorphisms) could provide an unexpected shortcut to a solution.

If we conclude that no fundamental speedup is possible, we want to be able to explain this convincingly—ideally formulating conditions or no-go theorems that exclude such a sub-\(\sqrt{n}\) breakthrough. Conversely, if we discover a new curve model, a new type of arithmetic, or a new attack—even if currently impractical—it will be a valuable advance in understanding the problem.

This is a long-term research program rather than a single result. Our aim is to document every step—ideas that work, ideas that fail, and the boundary between what seems mathematically possible and impossible for ECDLP.

Output Style

Project outputs will be prepared to be valuable to experts while accessible to the broader public interested in mathematics and cryptography.

Each topic will be examined in depth—first theoretically (including formal proofs), then experimentally (algorithm verification, measurements, demonstrations), and finally summarized in an understandable form.

We plan to prepare not only technical reports but also more popular articles (e.g., on this blog) that explain even complex concepts in a readable and illustrative way. The writing style emphasizes readability: we highlight important ideas, use clear text structure, graphs, and examples.

We want not only cryptographic experts but also motivated readers from the broader community to take away insights from our materials. This project has the ambition to inspire—to show the beauty hidden in the mathematics of elliptic curves and to stimulate further research at the intersection of algebraic number theory, geometry, and cryptography.
